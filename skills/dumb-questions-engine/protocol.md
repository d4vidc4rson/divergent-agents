# Ask Questions — Methodology

Generate questions, not answers. The quality of answers is capped by the quality of questions. A dumb question nobody's asking can unlock answers nobody's considered.

---

## Step 1: Name the Territory

The territory is not the problem -- it's the space the problem lives in. Go one level wider.

Questions about the problem produce incremental reframes. Questions about the territory produce paradigm shifts.

If the problem is "how do we increase our app's share rate," the territory is "how single-player mobile experiences become social."

If the problem is "how do we reduce hospital readmissions," the territory is "how people navigate the transition from institutional care to home."

State the territory in one sentence. That sentence is the only input for the rest of the skill.

---

## Step 2: Map the Assumptions

List 8-12 things that everyone in this territory takes for granted. Not debatable positions -- settled consensus. The stuff experts would say "well, obviously" about.

These are the things so obvious nobody says them out loud because questioning them feels stupid. That's exactly why they need questioning.

---

## Step 3: Generate the Questions

Take each assumption and generate 1-2 questions that challenge it. Use three patterns (mix them, don't label them):

### Pattern A: "What if the opposite were true?"

Take an assumption and flip it.

### Pattern B: "Why does this have to be this way?"

Ask whether it's a law of nature or just a habit.

### Pattern C: "What would a complete outsider find absurd?"

Look at an assumption from the perspective of someone who has never encountered this territory.

### Output

Generate 15-20 questions total. Write each as a single question. No preamble. No explanation. No "this is interesting because..." The question speaks for itself or it doesn't belong.

---

## Step 4: Star the Dangerous Ones

Apply the Expert Threat Test to every question:

Would a senior person in this territory hear this question and feel comfortable? If yes, the question is too safe -- it's a "good question" that stays inside the existing frame.

Would they feel a flash of defensiveness? Would they say "that's not how it works" or "you don't understand the field" or "we've been over this"? That defensiveness is the signal. The question touched something structural -- something the expert's career is built on.

Star 3-5 questions that produce the most defensiveness.

Present the full list of 15-20 questions with dangerous ones starred.

Then add a separator and ask:

**Which of the starred questions do you want to walk through? Pick one, pick several, or tell me which ones scare you -- those are usually right.**

The user must know it's their turn. The list is a menu. The user picks the doors.

---

## After Selection: Walking Through Doors

Each starred question the user selects can become:

- **A reframed problem** to feed into a generation skill
- **An input for the Wrong Problem Detector** if it suggests the user is solving the wrong problem
- **A seed for Think Wrong** -- assume the answer is yes and build the counterintuitive position
- **A prompt for Strip Down** if it points at a deeper desire than the user started with

---

## Quality Criteria

- Territory is one level wider than the stated problem, not the problem itself
- Assumptions are genuinely "obvious" settled consensus, not strawmen or controversial positions
- Questions challenge real structural assumptions, not surface-level practices
- At least 3 patterns (opposite, why, outsider) are represented in the mix
- Starred questions would make a domain expert defensive, not just thoughtful
- The list contains at least 15 questions with 3-5 starred

## Self-Check

1. Is the territory wider than the problem? (If it's the same scope, zoom out)
2. Would domain experts say "well, obviously" about each assumption? (If any are debatable, they're not assumptions -- they're opinions)
3. Do the starred questions challenge structural foundations, or just surface practices?
4. Would an expert feel a flash of defensiveness, or just nod thoughtfully? (If they'd nod, the question isn't dangerous enough)
5. Did you end with a clear handoff to the user to choose which doors to open?
