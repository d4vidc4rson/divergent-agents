# Audit Diversity — Methodology

Post-generation diversity audit. Takes any list of ideas and reveals how many genuinely different ideas exist underneath the surface variety.

This skill does three things: Cluster, Grade, Diagnose. It does not generate new ideas or fix the list.

---

## Step 1: Cluster

For every idea in the list, ask: **"What underlying move is this making?"**

Not what the idea says on the surface. What it's doing underneath.

### How to identify the underlying move:

- Look past the tactic to the mechanism.
- Two ideas that use different methods but make the same structural move belong in the same cluster.
- Two ideas that look similar on the surface but make different structural moves belong in different clusters.

### How to name the cluster:

State the move as a verb phrase. The name should be:

- Specific enough that you can immediately tell whether a new idea belongs in it
- Abstract enough that it describes the move rather than the medium

**Good cluster names:** verb phrases describing what the idea does
- "Get attention through borrowed audiences"
- "Search faster through the existing solution space"
- "Remove a step instead of improving it"
- "Change what we measure rather than what we do"

**Too vague:** "Marketing ideas," "Technical improvements," "Engagement"

**Too specific:** "TikTok ideas," "Microservices ideas," "Grant-funded approaches"

### Present the clusters:

Group every idea under its cluster name. Show the cluster name, the count of ideas in it, and each idea listed underneath.

---

## Step 2: Grade

Score overall diversity using this scale:

### Grade A -- Genuine portfolio
- 5+ distinct clusters
- No cluster contains more than 30% of the ideas
- Clusters address different problems, use different mechanisms, would appeal to different stakeholders

### Grade B -- Decent spread with one gravity well
- 4+ distinct clusters
- One cluster is overweight (40%+ of ideas)
- Variety exists but there's a clear center of gravity

### Grade C -- Illusion of variety
- 2-3 clusters
- Most ideas are doing the same thing in different clothes
- Feels like options but you're choosing between flavors of the same answer

### Grade D -- Monoculture
- 1-2 clusters
- Everything is the same idea exploring its own surface area
- Start over

### Present the grade:

State the grade. State the cluster count. State which cluster is heaviest. Be blunt about what the user actually has versus what they think they have.

---

## Step 3: Diagnose

Identify what's missing from the set. Don't generate new ideas -- point at the empty spaces.

### Check for these common gaps:

**Time horizon gaps**
- Are all ideas short-term? Is anything a long play? Is anything actionable this week?

**Mechanism gaps**
- Is everything the same type of action? All digital, nothing physical? All intervention, nothing structural? All about acquiring new, nothing about deepening existing?

**Audience gaps**
- Do all ideas target the same group? Is anyone being ignored who shouldn't be?

**Risk gaps**
- Is everything safe and implementable? Where are the stretch ideas and moonshots? Or conversely, is everything ambitious with nothing doable Tuesday?

**Problem gaps**
- Are all ideas solving the same sub-problem? What sub-problems does the list completely ignore?

### Present the gaps:

State 2-4 missing dimensions. Don't generate ideas to fill them. Point at the empty space and let the user decide whether to fill it.

---

## Quality Criteria

1. Every idea is assigned to exactly one cluster.
2. Cluster names are verb phrases at the right level of abstraction (not too vague, not too specific).
3. The grade accurately reflects the cluster distribution -- not inflated, not deflated.
4. The diagnosis identifies genuinely missing dimensions, not just restating what's already there in different words.
5. No new ideas are generated. This is audit only.

---

## Self-Check

1. Read each cluster name. Could two of them be merged without losing meaning? If so, merge them and re-grade.
2. Are any ideas misclustered? Would moving an idea to a different cluster change the grade? If so, move it.
3. Is the grade honest? If you showed this to the user and they pushed back, would you defend it? If not, adjust.
4. Do the diagnosed gaps point at genuinely different territory from what exists? Or are they just more specific versions of existing clusters?
5. Have you avoided generating solutions? The urge to "help" by suggesting ideas for the gaps is strong. Resist it. Diagnosis only.
