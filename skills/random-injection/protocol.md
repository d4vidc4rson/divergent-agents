# Inject Random — Methodology

Inject a genuinely random, unrelated concept into a problem and force deep structural connections -- mechanism transfer, not metaphor.

The distinction between mechanism transfer and metaphor is what makes this work instead of being a party trick. "Be more flexible like a jellyfish" is useless metaphor. "Distribute force across multiple shifting chambers rather than resisting with a rigid shell" is a transferable mechanism.

---

## Step 1: State the Problem

One sentence. The simpler the better.

Strip away qualifiers, segments, and specifics. Simple problem statements leave room for unexpected connections. Detailed ones anchor the transfer toward obvious bridges.

Not: "How do we increase customer retention across our B2B SaaS platform targeting mid-market companies in the healthcare vertical."
Instead: "How do we keep customers from leaving?"

---

## Step 2: Generate a Random Word

The word must be a **concrete, physical noun**. Not an abstract concept, emotion, or professional term.

Abstract words ("justice," "efficiency") don't have mechanisms to study -- they have meanings to interpret. Interpretation is what you're trying to escape.

Good sources: natural organisms, physical objects, geological formations. Nature is ideal because natural systems have evolved mechanisms over millions of years that human design hasn't discovered.

Examples: jellyfish, volcano, beehive, glacier, spider web, mangrove, coral reef, lightning, mushroom, termite mound, dandelion, stalactite, tidal pool, bamboo, lichen, whirlpool, pomegranate, sequoia, prairie fire

Selection method: Pick the first concrete noun that comes to mind. Or pick a random number 1-20 and count down a list. The method doesn't matter; the randomness does.

**Do not reject a word for being "too unrelated."** The more unrelated, the better. If the word seems connected to the problem, throw it out and pick again. Maximum distance between word and problem is where novel connections live.

---

## Step 3: Study the Word Deeply

This is the critical step. Do NOT free-associate from the word.

Wrong approach (free-association): "Jellyfish -> squishy -> flexible -> be more flexible!" This produces nothing you wouldn't think of anyway. The word added zero value.

Right approach (mechanism study): Ask **"How does this thing actually work? What are its mechanisms? What problems has it solved? What principles does it operate on?"**

Example for jellyfish:
- No brain, no heart, no bones -- survived 500 million years. **Principle:** Extreme simplicity can outperform complex systems over long time horizons.
- Moves by pulsing -- contracting then relaxing. **Principle:** Rhythmic alternation between effort and recovery is more efficient than constant effort.
- 95% water, nearly indistinguishable from environment. **Principle:** The less different you are from your surroundings, the less energy you spend on defense.
- Drifts with currents rather than fighting them. **Principle:** Using existing forces instead of generating your own can be a superior strategy.
- Sting is passive defense -- zero effort from the defender. **Principle:** Deterrence that requires no active effort.

Extract 3-5 principles per random word. Each principle should be stated as a general mechanism, not as a fact about the thing.

Not: "Jellyfish are 95% water."
Instead: "Being nearly indistinguishable from your environment reduces the energy cost of survival."

---

## Step 4: Extract Principles

Write each principle as a standalone statement that makes sense without knowing its source. The principle should be domain-agnostic and transferable.

List all 3-5 principles cleanly.

---

## Step 5: Transfer

For each principle, ask: **"If this principle were true in the world of my problem, what would that look like?"**

Generate a concrete idea or direction for the problem from each principle. The transfer should produce specific, actionable insights -- not vague analogies.

---

## Multiple Injections

If one word produces 3-5 interesting transfers, that's usually enough.

For more divergence, inject a second random word and repeat the full process. Two words applied to the same problem produce ideas in completely different regions of solution space because they have no relationship to each other.

Do not combine words. Run them separately. Each word is its own injection. Three words maximum before diminishing returns.

---

## Quality Criteria

- The random word is genuinely unrelated to the problem (not a sneaky relevant choice)
- Study goes deep into actual mechanisms, not surface-level word association
- Principles are stated as general, transferable mechanisms -- not facts about the source
- Transfers produce specific, concrete ideas -- not vague analogies ("be more like X")
- Each transfer would not have been generated through normal problem-solving
- The scaffolding (the random word, the organism) falls away -- the principle stands on its own

## Self-Check

1. Could someone read the principles without knowing the source word and still find them useful?
2. Are the transfers specific enough to act on, or are they just metaphors?
3. Did the study phase go beyond the first obvious association? (If you stopped at "jellyfish = flexible," go deeper)
4. Would these ideas have emerged from any structured brainstorming method? (If yes, the injection didn't add enough distance)
5. Is there at least one transfer that genuinely surprises you?
