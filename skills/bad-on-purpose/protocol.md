# Generate Bad — Methodology

Two phases. Never combine them. Phase 1 is generation. Phase 2 is excavation. They use opposite cognitive muscles.

---

## Phase 1: Be Terrible

Generate 5-7 ideas that are genuinely, intentionally bad. Not quirky. Not unconventional. Not "crazy but it might work." Actually bad. If you read it and think "well, there's something to this," it's not bad enough.

### Rules

- The idea must be genuinely bad -- push until it's embarrassing
- The discomfort of writing it is the signal you've gotten past the quality filter
- Each idea should be bad in a different way
- Write each bad idea as one sentence. No explanation. No defense. No "I know this sounds crazy but..."

### Flavors of Bad

Each flavor violates a different category of assumption:

- **Ethically bad:** "What if we lied?"
- **Logistically insane:** "What if it took 400 years?"
- **Financially suicidal:** "What if we paid people and never charged?"
- **Professionally unacceptable:** "What if we insulted our audience?"
- **Physically impossible:** "What if it could read minds?"
- **Comically lazy:** "What if we just didn't solve it and called it a feature?"
- **Career-ending:** "What if we publicly admitted everything wrong?"

Don't pick a flavor on purpose. Let ideas be bad in whatever way they want.

---

## Phase 2: Mine the Wreckage

For each terrible idea, ask three questions:

### Question 1: "What assumption does this violate?"

Every bad idea is bad because it breaks a rule. Name the rule. The rule it breaks is the assumption worth examining -- not because it's wrong, but because assumptions that never get examined become invisible walls constraining every "good" idea.

### Question 2: "Is there a 5% version of this that isn't bad?"

The idea is 100% bad. But somewhere between 0% and 100% there's a version that's just weird enough to be interesting and just viable enough to work.

The 5% version is almost always more interesting than anything the quality filter would have produced, because it starts from a place the filter would never allow.

### Question 3: "What door did this open?"

Forget the idea. What direction did it point? What territory did it reveal that you didn't know was there? Sometimes a bad idea doesn't have a 5% version -- it's just bad all the way down. But it pointed somewhere. Name the door, even if you don't walk through it right now.

---

## Output Structure

For each terrible idea, present:
- **The bad idea** (one sentence)
- **The assumption it breaks** (one sentence)
- **The 5% version** (one sentence -- or "no viable version, but...")
- **The door it opens** (one sentence)

Then collect the 5% versions and opened doors into a clean list. These are the actual outputs. The bad ideas were the tool. The 5% versions and doors are what you take forward.

---

## Quality Criteria

- Bad ideas are genuinely cringe-worthy, not just mildly unconventional
- Each bad idea violates a different type of assumption
- Assumptions identified are real constraints the domain takes for granted, not strawmen
- 5% versions are genuinely interesting and non-obvious -- not just "the safe version"
- Doors opened point to real unexplored territory, not platitudes
- The final collected list contains actionable seeds, not just cleverness

## Self-Check

1. Would you be embarrassed to show the Phase 1 ideas to an expert? (If not, they're not bad enough)
2. Do the broken assumptions name real rules the domain operates by?
3. Are the 5% versions ideas you would NOT have generated through normal brainstorming?
4. Does the collected output list contain at least 3 genuinely novel directions?
5. Did Phase 2 extract value from ideas that Phase 1 would have killed?
