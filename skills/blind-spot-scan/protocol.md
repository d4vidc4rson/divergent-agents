# Blind Spot Scan Protocol

Map the full problem space to identify what you forgot to think about. Use before generation (to ensure coverage) or after generation (to reveal gaps). Diverse ideas can still be incomplete if every cluster lives in the same region of the map.

---

## Two Modes

**Pre-generation mode:** Run before brainstorming. Produce a map of the full problem space. Generation skills then consciously address different regions.

**Post-generation mode:** Run after brainstorming. Lay existing ideas on the map and show which zones are empty.

Same map either way. The difference is timing.

---

## Building the Map: Seven Dimensions

Scan all seven. Not every problem touches all seven, but scanning all ensures you see what's missing before deciding what matters.

### 1. Who's Affected?

List every distinct group the problem touches. Not just the obvious target -- everyone.

Think beyond the primary audience: the implementers, the recipients, the funders, the inheritors, the adjacent groups affected by spillover, the people who must say yes, and the people whose cooperation is assumed but not secured.

### 2. What Resources Already Exist?

List what's already in play that ideas could build on, redirect, or repurpose:

- Funding or budget already flowing
- People already engaged (how many, how deeply)
- Relationships already built (partners, allies, collaborators)
- Infrastructure already in place (systems, tools, spaces, programs)
- Credibility, reputation, or trust already earned
- Data, research, or knowledge already collected
- Content, publications, or materials already created
- Equipment, facilities, or physical assets already available

Most brainstorms invent from scratch. The map shows what's already there.

### 3. What Time Horizons Are in Play?

- **This week** -- What needs to happen immediately?
- **This quarter** -- What could show results in 90 days?
- **This year** -- What plays out over 6-12 months?
- **Multi-year** -- What's the 3-5 year structural play?

Most brainstorms cluster in one time horizon. The map forces visibility across all four.

### 4. What Strategic Postures Are Available?

- **Offense** -- Grow, build, expand, create new
- **Defense** -- Protect what you have, retain, prevent loss
- **Bridge** -- Survive the gap, buy time, temporary measures
- **Retreat** -- Cut, shrink, consolidate, refocus
- **Transform** -- Change what you are, new model, new identity

Most brainstorms are pure offense. The map forces the question: what if growth doesn't come fast enough?

### 5. What Types of Value Are Available?

- **Material value** -- Money, resources, physical things
- **Knowledge value** -- Data, insight, expertise, research
- **Relationship value** -- Trust, alliances, networks, community
- **Capability value** -- Skills built, capacity created, infrastructure developed
- **Attention value** -- Awareness, visibility, credibility, influence
- **Time value** -- Speed gained, delays prevented, optionality preserved

### 6. What Mechanisms Could Deliver Ideas?

- **Direct intervention** -- Doing the thing yourself, hands-on
- **System or process change** -- Changing how things are structured or governed
- **Communication or narrative** -- Changing what's said, published, taught, or framed
- **Experience or environment** -- Changing what people encounter physically or socially
- **Partnership or collaboration** -- Working through or with others
- **Technology or tools** -- Building or deploying systems
- **Incentive or policy** -- Changing the rules, rewards, or costs

### 7. What Could Go Wrong?

- What happens if the primary strategy takes longer than expected?
- What happens if the target audience doesn't respond as hoped?
- What external events could change the landscape?
- What internal constraints could bottleneck execution?
- What dependencies could break?

---

## Pre-Generation Mode Output

1. Run the seven dimensions against the desire statement or brief.
2. Produce the map as a clean list of what exists in each dimension.
3. Don't generate ideas. Generate the territory.
4. Tell the user what the map contains and ask: "Which regions do you want to prioritize? And which are you comfortable leaving empty?"
5. Feed the prioritized regions into whatever generation skill is used.

---

## Post-Generation Mode Output

1. Take the existing set of ideas.
2. Build the map.
3. Lay each idea on the map and mark which regions it covers.
4. Present a coverage report with three sections:

**Covered:** Which dimensions and regions have at least one idea addressing them.

**Empty:** Which dimensions and regions have zero ideas. These are the blind spots.

**Overweight:** Which regions have 3+ ideas. These are gravity wells -- places thinking naturally goes that may pull attention from regions that matter more.

---

## After the Scan: Triage and Fill

### 1. Ask the user to pick

Present blind spots as a pick list. Not every blind spot matters -- some are critical, some are irrelevant.

Ask: "Which 2-3 feel like they'd actually change your thinking if they had ideas in them? The rest we'll leave empty on purpose."

Frame it as a choice, not a homework assignment. Some gaps should stay empty intentionally.

### 2. Turn each chosen blind spot into a question

Convert each selected gap into a direct, plain-language question. One question per blind spot. No frameworks.

### 3. Generate immediately into the chosen gaps

Do not ask which generation skill to use. Just go. Use the skill that fits the gap's complexity. Merge new ideas with the original set.

---

## Quality Criteria

1. All seven dimensions are scanned, even if some are marked as not applicable.
2. The map is specific to this problem, not a generic template with blanks filled in.
3. Coverage report clearly distinguishes covered, empty, and overweight regions.
4. Blind spots identified are genuinely missing territory, not restatements of existing clusters.
5. The user is asked to choose which gaps matter before any gap-filling begins.
6. Gap-filling questions are plain language and directly actionable.

---

## Self-Check

1. Did you scan all seven dimensions, or did you skip the ones that felt less relevant? Scan all seven -- the one you'd skip is often the one that catches the blind spot.
2. Is the "Who's Affected" list comprehensive? Most blind spots live here. If you listed fewer than 4 groups, you probably missed some.
3. Are strategic postures represented beyond offense? If the map shows only growth/build/expand ideas, defense and bridge are almost certainly blind spots.
4. Is the coverage report honest about what's overweight? Gravity wells are uncomfortable to name because they're where the user's energy already is.
5. After gap-filling, do the new ideas cover genuinely different territory, or did they cluster back into existing regions?
