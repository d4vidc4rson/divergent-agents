# MacGyver Mode Protocol

Force creative solutions using only resources already available. Three steps: Inventory, Reframe, Combine.

Constraints force recombination. Recombination produces novelty. The fewer options you have, the more creative the combinations become.

---

## Step 1: Inventory

List everything the user actually has. Not what they wish they had. What exists right now, today. Be specific -- vague resources suggest vague ideas; specific resources suggest specific combinations.

### Inventory Categories

**People and expertise**
Who is accessible? Not just staff -- colleagues, advisors, former team members, students, community members, volunteers. What skills do they have that aren't currently being used? What relationships haven't been tapped?

**Existing audience or constituency**
Who already pays attention? How many? Where? How engaged? Include people who showed up once and never came back but are still reachable.

**Knowledge and content**
What's already been created or collected? Publications, datasets, research, curricula, documentation, frameworks, presentations, recorded sessions, internal reports. What's sitting in archives that could be repurposed?

**Technology, tools, and infrastructure**
What systems and tools already have access? What's set up and running? What are you paying for but only using 10% of? What equipment sits idle? What software has unexplored features?

**Relationships and partnerships**
Who already knows you? Who owes a favor? Who would take your call? What organizations are you already connected to?

**Physical assets and spaces**
What spaces, equipment, materials are available? What facilities go underused at certain hours or seasons?

**Budget already flowing**
What funding or revenue exists? What budget line items could be redirected? What spending isn't working and could be reallocated?

**Credibility, reputation, and trust**
What are you known for? What do people trust you to do? What authority exists that isn't being deployed?

### Specificity Requirement

Not "we have a website." Instead: "a website with 4,200 monthly visitors, a blog with 47 posts, and an email list of 1,800 people with a 28% open rate."

Not "we have a lab." Instead: "a lab with two unused PCR machines, a postdoc between projects for three weeks, and an unanalyzed dataset from last year's pilot."

---

## Step 2: Reframe

For each inventory item, ask: **"What else could this be?"**

Every resource has a default use (what it was created for) and 3-4 non-default uses nobody considers because the default is so obvious.

For each item in the inventory, list 2-3 non-default uses. Don't evaluate whether they're good. Just name them.

The reframe is where the creative value emerges. When you see an asset as something other than its default purpose, entirely different strategies become possible without spending a dollar.

---

## Step 3: Combine

Generate ideas using ONLY inventory items -- but in non-obvious combinations.

### The Rule

Every idea must use at least two inventory items together in a way they haven't been used before.

One resource in its default use = business as usual. Two resources combined in a new way = an invention built from scraps.

### How to Force Combinations

Pick two items from the inventory that seem unrelated. Ask: "What happens if I combine these?"

Generate 8-10 combination ideas. Each must draw from the actual inventory. No new resources allowed. If an idea requires something not on the inventory list, it doesn't belong in this session.

---

## Quality Criteria

- Inventory is specific and concrete, not vague hand-waving
- Reframes reveal genuinely non-obvious uses, not trivially adjacent ones
- Every combination idea uses only resources from the inventory -- no smuggled-in new resources
- Ideas combine at least two inventory items in a novel way
- At least half the ideas could realistically be executed within weeks
- The constraint (only existing resources) is honored strictly throughout

## Self-Check

1. Is the inventory specific enough that a stranger could verify each item exists?
2. Do the reframes reveal uses the user likely hasn't considered?
3. Does every idea pass the "no new resources" test?
4. Are combinations genuinely novel, not just two default uses stapled together?
5. Could the user start on at least 3 of these ideas this week?
6. Did any combination surprise you? (If nothing surprised you, the combinations aren't creative enough)
